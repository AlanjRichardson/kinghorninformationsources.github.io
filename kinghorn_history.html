History

Introduction

The first human inhabitants appeared on the shores of the Firth of Forth about 10,000 years ago they were hunter-gatherers. Later, the Neolithic Picts arrived and introduced herding and cultivation to the area. Kinghorn, with its fertile land, abundant, fresh, water, and access to marine resources, was likely a tribal settlement for centuries. Eventually, the Scots replaced the tribes and united the country, creating the Scottish monarchy.

Kinghorn rose to prominence around the early/mid 13th century when it became a favoured royal hunting ground and a castle was built, there is a question of where it was built but the weight of evidence suggests it was near the loch burn on the rising gound where Burt Avenue is today. Kinghorn received a confirmatory charter from King Alexander 111 in 1285 elevating the village to one of the earliest Royal Burghs in Scotland. Tragically in 1286 the King was killed when he fell off his horse whilst returning from Edinburgh to Kinghorn Castle to be with his wife Yolande. It was said that one Murdoch Schank (Schanck) discovered and looked after King Alexander 111's body. The Schank family were an ancient family who came from the Land of Schank in Midlothian. It is also said that Robert the Bruce who reigned from 1306 - 1329 awarded Murdoch Schank the estate of Castlerigg in Kinghorn in recognition of this service to the throne. Murdoch and his family appear to have taken up residence in Kinghorn in 1319. The genealogy of the Schanks from then until 1884 can be accessed see the links below.

Kinghorn Castle was eventually gifted to the Lyon family (Glamis Castle Angus) in the 15th century which is probably when it became known as Glamis Tower/Castle. The castle then swapped hands several times and during this period appears to have been used as a defensive position by French troops in a skirmish with reformation forces.
